# Market & Landscape Research — Excurse

**Research Lead: Market & Landscape · July 2026**
**Scope:** The 2025–2026 travel-planning landscape across four clusters (AI planners, classic organizers, bespoke human services, editorial guides), the failure modes Excurse must avoid, and where a private, offline, encrypted, editorial, composed-for-you field guide actually sits — and could go.

---

## 0. Executive framing

The travel-planning market in mid-2026 is loud, converging, and strangely hollow in exactly the place Excurse lives. Every well-funded player is racing toward the same endpoint: a conversational booking funnel. Meanwhile two counter-signals are strengthening: (1) travelers — including 38–60% of Gen Z/millennials in recent surveys — are returning to *human* advisors precisely because AI output feels generic and untrustworthy; and (2) print/editorial guide brands (Wildsam, Herb Lester, Monocle, Atlas Obscura) keep proving people pay for *voice, curation, and object-quality*, not inventory.

Excurse's category — **a composed artifact, not a planning tool** — has no direct competitor. Everyone else optimizes the *transaction* (booking commissions) or the *process* (collaborative planning UX). Nobody ships the *result*: a finished, private, beautiful, offline thing made for named people. That's the position to defend.

## 1. Market context: adoption, money, and the agentic turn

- **Adoption is mainstream but shallow.** ~40% of global travelers used AI tools for planning in 2025; 62% of millennials/Gen Z in key markets. Skift Research: about one-third of U.S. travelers use AI assistants "extensively" to research/plan/book; McKinsey says over half use ChatGPT-class tools at least occasionally. Top use cases are research (54%), inspiration and food recs (43% each) — i.e., *upstream* of the trip, not *during* it. ([Travala 2026 stats](https://www.travala.com/blog/how-many-travelers-use-ai-for-booking-key-insights-for-2026/), [McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/featured-insights/week-in-charts/travel-planning-gets-an-ai-upgrade), [Skift](https://skift.com/2025/08/01/booking-trends-ai-tools-and-the-new-rules-of-travel-planning/))
- **Generative-AI-in-travel market**: ~$2.7B (2025) → projected ~$18B by 2035 (~21% CAGR). AI-driven referral traffic grew 187% in 2025, heavily concentrated in travel/retail/media. ([MarketResearchFuture](https://www.marketresearchfuture.com/reports/generative-ai-in-travel-market-31666))
- **The agentic turn (Oct 2025–):** OpenAI launched in-ChatGPT apps with **Expedia and Booking.com** as flagship partners (Apps SDK); Operator launched earlier with Tripadvisor, Booking, Priceline, Uber, Hipcamp. ChatGPT now surfaces live prices/availability and moves toward in-chat booking ("agentic commerce"). ([Skift](https://skift.com/2025/10/06/expedia-booking-chatgpt-apps-openai/), [PhocusWire](https://www.phocuswire.com/openai-chatgpt-apps-expedia-booking-tripadvisor), [OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/booking-com/))
- **Countercurrent — the human resurgence:** Chase Travel survey (4,000+ travelers 18–60): **60% of Gen Z/millennials want to use a travel advisor**; 38% of Gen Z already use traditional agents. Virtuoso's 2025: sales +12% H1 YoY, $50k+ bookings **+63%**, average sold hotel ADR hit $2,158/night in Dec 2025. The very tools meant to replace advisors are sending people back to them. ([Luxury Travel Advisor](https://www.luxurytraveladvisor.com/your-business/chase-travel-survey-60-gen-z-and-millennial-travelers-want-use-travel-advisors), [Travel Weekly](https://www.travelweekly.com/Travel-News/Travel-Agent-Issues/Insights/Virtuoso-puts-a-bow-on-2025))

**Read:** the market has split into "cheap generic AI planning" and "expensive human care." The middle — AI-scale *composition* with human-grade *taste and intimacy* — is open. That is Excurse's lane.

## 2. Cluster A — AI trip planners

### Mindtrip ($22M raised; Capital One, United Airlines, AMEX Ventures)
- **What it does well:** the most fully-featured AI planner; 11M+ POI database grounded against real listings; visual itineraries with maps; May 2026 launched conversation-led **flight booking with Sabre + PayPal agentic commerce** — first "all-in-one agentic AI flight booking." Fast Company "Most Innovative" 2025. ([PhocusWire](https://www.phocuswire.com/ai-travel-planner-mindtrip-receipts-funding), [Sabre](https://investors.sabre.com/news-releases/news-release-details/mindtrip-launches-travels-first-all-one-agentic-ai-flight))
- **Business model:** free to consumers; **booking commissions** + a creator program ($1–1.50/registered user, commissions via shared "magic links") + B2B licensing. Revenue gravity = transactions, so the product must always steer toward bookable inventory.
- **Where it's hollow:** everything is optimized toward the funnel. Itineraries read as competent aggregations, not compositions. No offline story, no privacy story, no "made for *you specifically*" feeling — personalization is filters, not understanding. The traveler is a lead.

### Layla.ai (~1.1M users)
- **Well:** live pricing that actually works (BudgetAir/Skyscanner integration), real bookable tickets, PriceLock fare tracking, memory of your name/preferences. ([Layla](https://layla.ai/), [aitravel.tools review](https://aitravel.tools/layla-ai-review/))
- **Model:** freemium; premium $9.99/mo or $49.99/yr; PDF export paywalled; recurring Trustpilot complaints about surprise charges post-trial.
- **Hollow:** consumer-chat gloss over an OTA. The "personality" is a marketing skin; recommendations converge on the popular. Dark-pattern billing erodes exactly the trust a travel companion needs.

### Wanderlog
- **Well:** the best *collaborative* planner — Google-Docs-style simultaneous editing, clean UX, route optimization, strong free tier. Genuinely loved app. ([Wanderlog reviews](https://www.wandrly.app/reviews/wanderlog))
- **Model:** Pro ~$39.99–49.99/yr; **offline mode and AI assistant are paywalled** — users are vocally frustrated that a travel app doesn't work offline by default. ([Pricing analysis](https://monkeyeatingmango.com/blog/wanderlog-pricing-2026/))
- **Hollow:** itinerary-as-spreadsheet. It organizes what *you* found; it has no taste of its own. The output looks like a database view, never like a guide. Note well: Wanderlog charging for offline proves offline is *valued* — Excurse gives it away as a first principle.

### Google (Search AI Overviews, Gemini, Maps)
- **Well:** "create an itinerary for Costa Rica" in AI Overviews with photos/maps/export-to-Maps; Gemini Gems trip planners free for everyone; **"Ask Maps"** Gemini integration recommending from Maps data + reviews; hotel price tracking. Distribution nobody can match. ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/03/27/google-rolls-out-new-vacation-planning-features-to-search-maps-and-gemini/), [Google blog](https://blog.google/products-and-platforms/products/search/summer-travel-tips-ai-overviews-hotel-price-tracking/))
- **Hollow:** maximally generic by construction — it's popularity data all the way down. No persistence, no narrative, no relationship. Also the graveyard risk: Google My Maps and saved lists remain clunky, half-maintained planning surfaces; Google historically abandons this layer.

### ChatGPT / Operator-style agents (+ Expedia/Booking apps)
- **Well:** open-ended research is genuinely good with heavy prompting; Deep Research produces impressive dossiers; in-chat live inventory via partner apps is now real in the US and several markets. ([Expedia in ChatGPT](https://www.expedia.com/product/expedia-in-chatgpt/))
- **Hollow:** the output is chat transcript + links — evaporates, isn't an artifact, doesn't work on a plane, and (see §6) hallucinates places and hours with documented real-world harm.

### OTA incumbents: Expedia & Airbnb
- **Expedia (Explore 2026):** Activity Planner (NL → bookable itinerary), Hotels.com AI Compare, Property Expert Q&A, "Romie" the group-chat sidekick; heavy B2B API push. ([Expedia Newsroom](https://www.expedia.com/newsroom/expedia-group-unveils-new-ai-experiences-expands-travel-ecosystem-and-launches-philanthropy-program-at-explore-2026/), [WiT](https://www.webintravel.com/expedia-group-launches-new-ai-powered-trip-planner-and-multiple-apis/))
- **Airbnb (summer 2026):** AI review-highlights synthesized from 1B+ reviews, AI wishlist comparison, collaborative group itinerary builder, Trips-tab map with nearby restaurants/experiences — explicit expansion beyond rentals. ([Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/travel/2026/05/21/airbnb-expands-beyond-rentals-with-ai-planning-tools-airport-pickups-and-world-cup-experie), [Gadget Hacks](https://smartphones.gadgethacks.com/news/airbnb-app-update-2026-ai-planning-group-trips-and-global-support/))
- **Hollow:** every feature exists to increase attach rate on *their inventory*. Personalization = conversion optimization. The moment your trip involves things they can't sell (a grandmother's house, a free neighborhood walk, a kid's nap window), they lose interest.

**Cluster A verdict:** enormously capitalized, structurally incapable of Excurse's job. Their unit of value is the booking; Excurse's unit of value is the *trip as lived by specific people*. None ship an offline artifact; none are private (most are surveillance-adjacent by business model); none have editorial voice.

## 3. Cluster B — Classic tools

### TripIt / TripIt Pro ($49/yr, SAP-owned)
- **Well:** still the best at one thing — forward confirmation emails, get a clean master itinerary, offline. Pro adds real-time flight alerts, gate calls, seat tracking. ([Going review](https://www.going.com/guides/tripit-review), [TripIt pricing](https://www.tripit.com/web/pro/pricing))
- **Hollow:** it organizes *logistics you already bought*. Zero soul, zero place-knowledge, dated UI, and its core workflow (email parsing) is being commoditized by free AI tools. It's a filing cabinet, not a guide. Also requires handing your inbox to SAP — a privacy contrast Excurse can draw sharply.

### Notion templates / Google Sheets / My Maps
- **Well:** infinitely flexible, free-to-cheap, huge template marketplace (Notion travel planners with embedded maps, budget trackers). This is what design-literate DIY planners actually use. ([Notion Marketplace](https://www.notion.com/templates/category/travel-planner))
- **Hollow:** *you* are the compositor. Hours of manual assembly; fragile offline behavior; no map-native experience; generic aesthetics; nothing adapts during the trip. My Maps is functional but neglected. The existence of a thriving template economy is evidence people crave a *structured, beautiful trip document* — they're just forced to build it themselves. Excurse is what these people wish existed.

**Cluster B verdict:** validates two demands Excurse satisfies natively — (1) one trustworthy offline place for everything, (2) a document-like, personal artifact — while showing the ceiling of tools that have no intelligence and no voice.

## 4. Cluster C — Bespoke human services

### Black Tomato (~$200M valuation, trips from ~$2,000/person to six figures)
- **Well:** emotion-first briefs ("Get Lost" wilderness drops, "Blink" pop-up camps), storytelling-grade itinerary design, service from first inquiry to post-return, community/conservation partnerships. The industry's proof that people pay premium prices for *narrative and feeling*, not logistics. ([Men's Journal](https://www.mensjournal.com/travel/black-tomato-review), [Silicon Review profile](https://thesiliconreview.com/magazine/profile/blacktomato-transformative-alchemy-luxury-travel-tourism))
- **Hollow:** opaque pricing until deep in consultation; doesn't scale below the luxury bracket; the deliverable during travel is often… a PDF and a WhatsApp number. The *artifact* is weaker than the service.

### Journy ($25/travel-day planning-only; $50/day with reservations)
- **Well:** the closest historical analog to Excurse-as-service: pair a traveler 1-on-1 with a designer, ask about tastes, deliver a custom day-by-day itinerary sourced from chef/hotelier networks. Transparent per-day pricing is a useful benchmark. ([Journy](https://www.gojourny.com/), [Hitha review](https://www.hithaonthego.com/review-journy-travel-planning/))
- **Hollow:** the intake was a form, not a conversation; the deliverable was an app/doc with modest design; economics of human designers at $25/day are brutal (the company has struggled to scale). The lesson: the *model* (interview → compose → deliver) resonates; the margin needs AI leverage and the artifact needs craft.

### Virtuoso network (20,000+ advisors, invitation-only, 2,200+ partners)
- **Well:** perks (upgrades, credits, breakfast) at no cost to traveler because **hotels pay the commission**; deep human relationships; booming (see §1). ([Upgraded Points explainer](https://upgradedpoints.com/travel/hotels/virtuoso-travel/))
- **Hollow:** advisor quality is wildly variable; the deliverable is emails and PDFs; commission incentives bias toward commissionable inventory (the same structural bias as OTAs, in human form); nothing works offline or feels made.

**Cluster C verdict:** demand for composed-for-you travel is real, growing, and increasingly younger. But every human service under-delivers on the *artifact* — the thing in your hand during the trip. Excurse inverts that: the artifact IS the product.

## 5. Cluster D — Editorial guides

- **Wildsam** (founded 2012, 50+ field guides, ~$20 each): "true stories of place" — essays, interviews, hand-drawn maps, almanac miscellany; closer to indie zines than Fodor's; expanded into a road-trip magazine in 2024. Texas Monthly called it "the next great American travel brand." Wildsam deliberately kept 99% of content in print — scarcity and object-quality as strategy. ([Wildsam](https://www.wildsam.com/), [Texas Monthly](https://www.texasmonthly.com/travel/wildsam-next-great-travel-brand/))
- **Herb Lester**: charming folded paper maps, "guides to the usual and unusual," tight curation (~50 spots), strong graphic identity, sold as gifts/objects.
- **Monocle Travel Guides**: city guides by local bureaus for the globally minded; opinionated, design-forward, brand-halo economics (the guide sells the magazine/brand and vice versa).
- **Atlas Obscura**: media + experiences; trips/experiences were 50–60% of revenue pre-2020, plus brand partnerships, courses, and membership ($100 trip credit, newsletter); turned its **first annual profit in 16 years** recently — proof that place-obsessed editorial can monetize through *experiences layered on content*. ([Digiday](https://digiday.com/media/atlas-obscura-redefines-exploration-after-pandemic-upturned-coverage-areas/), [A Media Operator](https://www.amediaoperator.com/newsletter/atlas-obscura-ads-are-crushing-but-whats-the-story-with-experiences/))

**What they do well:** voice, restraint, curation-as-signal ("only 17 stops, and every one earned"), beauty people keep on shelves. **Where they're hollow:** static, written for everyone (not for *you*), unaware of your dates/kids/knees/reservations, and print can't navigate, adapt, or hold a QR code.

**Cluster D verdict:** this is Excurse's *aesthetic* lineage and the strongest evidence for its thesis: people pay $20 for a paper guide to a city they'll share with a million other readers. A guide with that editorial quality, composed for *your family, your week, your pace* — with maps that navigate and tickets that scan, offline — is a strict superset. Excurse is "Wildsam, if Wildsam knew your name and your flight time."

## 6. Failure modes Excurse must design against

Documented, not hypothetical:

1. **Hallucinated places and hours.** 2025–26 incidents: tourists searching for a fictitious Andean canyon in Peru; a couple stranded on a Japanese mountain after AI-invented route info; pervasive wrong opening hours because models guess "usual" schedules. ([Futurism](https://futurism.com/artificial-intelligence/ai-hallucination-landmarks-tourists), [HuffPost](https://www.huffpost.com/entry/chatgpt-travel-plans-itinerary-trip_l_687107c9e4b00de383c0cf1f), [Copyleaks](https://copyleaks.com/blog/the-dangers-of-using-ai-travel-planner))
   → *Excurse rule:* every venue, hour, and route in a shipped guide is **verified against ground truth at compose time**, stamped with a "verified as of" date, and the compose pipeline treats unverifiable claims as blockers, not footnotes. A hand-made guide with one fake restaurant is dead.
2. **Generic top-10 slop / the flattening.** AI trained on popularity data reroutes everyone to the same over-touristed spots; "hidden gems" is literally a trigger phrase for the most generic output mode; CNN's test of ChatGPT city guides found them hollow. ([Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/next/2026/04/11/how-will-ai-impact-tourism-and-travel-your-next-trip-could-be-entirely-planned-by-chatgpt), [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/travel/ai-chatgpt-llms-travel-planning))
   → *Excurse rule:* the interview → traveler-model → compose loop exists precisely to escape this. Compose from *who these people are* (allergies, naps, "I just want your lean"), not from what's popular. Editorial constraint (few stops, strong opinions, a reason for each) is the anti-slop mechanism.
3. **Itinerary-as-spreadsheet.** Wanderlog/TripIt prove that a grid of times and links is organizationally useful and emotionally inert. → Excurse's day-narrative, themed days ("Marine Layer," "K-Town Neon"), LEAVE BY times, and "Nothing scheduled, exactly as planned" are the differentiator; never regress to tables.
4. **Booking-funnel gravity.** Commission models (Mindtrip, Layla, OTAs, even Virtuoso) structurally bias recommendations toward sellable inventory. → If Excurse ever monetizes, avoid commissions on content; charge for composition/craft (Journy/Wildsam pricing logic, not Expedia's).
5. **Subscription resentment & dark patterns.** Layla's surprise charges, Wanderlog paywalling offline. → Offline, privacy, and the whole guide are table stakes of the artifact, never upsells.
6. **Privacy hollowness.** Every mainstream tool wants your inbox, your location history, your graph. Excurse's client-side encryption (PBKDF2/AES-GCM, per-trip repos, no server that can read the trip) is not a feature bullet — it's the trust substrate that makes an *intimate* interview (allergies, a child, mobility limits, what you're celebrating) ethically possible. No competitor can honestly ask those questions.
7. **The abandoned-guide problem.** Static output rots (hours change, places close). → Guides should carry compose-date provenance and a lightweight re-verify/re-issue path; stale-while-revalidate already points the right way technically.

## 7. Positioning: what Excurse actually is

Map the landscape on two axes — **inventory-driven ↔ person-driven** and **process/tool ↔ finished artifact**:

- OTAs + AI planners: inventory-driven tools.
- TripIt/Notion/Wanderlog: person-neutral tools.
- Black Tomato/Journy/Virtuoso: person-driven, but the artifact is weak (PDFs, emails).
- Wildsam/Herb Lester/Monocle: finished artifacts, but for everyone.
- **Excurse: person-driven finished artifact. The quadrant is empty.**

**The differentiated claims (each individually rare, jointly unique):**
1. **Composed, not planned.** The traveler receives a finished editorial guide; they never touch a planning tool. "Asks nothing of the traveler" is the inverse of every competitor's engagement-maximizing design.
2. **Private by construction.** Invite-only, passphrase, client-side AES-GCM, no readable server copy, no account, no tracking. Uniquely credible place to hold intimate context.
3. **Offline-first as a promise, not a Pro feature.** Works in airplane mode, in the subway, in the canyon — the exact moments travel apps fail.
4. **Editorial voice.** Quiet, serene, specific ("Every loose end is tied. Enjoy the quiet."). Taste is the product; restraint is the feature.
5. **The interview.** "A story tells me more than a checklist ever could" — eliciting constraints (allergies, child, walking limits), rhythms (early/late, rest), and *stakes* (celebrate / get away / reunion) is a fundamentally richer input than any preference-filter UI shipping today. The academic literature (knowledge-graph traveler models blending long-term identity with short-term trip intent, e.g. [KGNARec-style work](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S095741742500716X), [interpretable KG decision-making](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0306457323001061)) supports formalizing this as a traveler/trip knowledge graph: entities (people, constraints, tastes, places, times) + relations, built conversationally, used at compose time — no consumer product does this today.

**The moat: taste + intimacy + craft, not inventory.**
- *Taste* can't be scraped: it's the compose-time editorial judgment plus a growing private corpus of what worked for whom.
- *Intimacy* compounds: each trip enriches a traveler model competitors can never see (encrypted, consented, per-person). Second-trip quality is the retention engine.
- *Craft* is the visible proof: typography, themed days, the wallet, the quiet copy. It's slow to copy because it's a thousand small decisions, and the funded players are incentivized not to bother (craft doesn't raise attach rate).
- Structural protection: commission-funded incumbents *can't* pivot here without abandoning their revenue model; editorial brands lack the software; human services lack the margin. The moat is a business-model moat as much as a product one.

**What Excurse is not (say it explicitly in strategy docs):** not a booking engine, not a social/creator platform, not a general chatbot, not a SaaS planner with seats and dashboards.

## 8. Plausible paths

**Stage 1 — Personal tool (now).** Trips for family/friends. Goal: harden the compose pipeline (interview → traveler graph → research → verify → compose → encrypt → deploy), accumulate 10–20 real trips, extract the reusable engine from the per-trip repos. Cost ≈ time + inference.

**Stage 2 — Boutique service (the natural next step).** "A field guide, composed for you." Invite/referral-only, N trips per month. Human (Richard + AI team) in the loop on every guide — the AI does research scale, the human does final taste. Pricing anchors from this research: Journy charged $25–50/travel-day for far less craft; Wildsam gets $20 for a generic paper guide; Virtuoso clients pay $2,000+/night hotels; Black Tomato starts at $2,000/person. A composed Excurse guide plausibly prices at **$150–600 per trip** (tiered by trip length/depth), with repeat clients as the core. This stage funds itself, generates the taste corpus, and — crucially — tests whether strangers value it, without scaling risk. The Chase/Virtuoso data (§1) says the demographic tailwind is real.
- Sub-path worth testing: **the gift.** A composed private guide for someone's honeymoon/anniversary/reunion is a near-perfect gift product (Black Tomato's emotional framing + Herb Lester's giftability), and gifts tolerate premium pricing.

**Stage 3 — Product (only if the service proves the loop).** Two honest options:
- **(a) Guide-as-a-service platform:** the composer tooling stays internal; scale by adding vetted "composers" (writers/planners with taste) using Excurse's pipeline — an atelier model (closer to Virtuoso's network than to SaaS), revenue share per guide.
- **(b) Self-serve compose:** the interview + compose loop productized for prosumers. Highest risk: this is where generic-slop gravity, verification cost, and support burden live, and where funded competitors could eventually collide. Only defensible if the traveler-graph + verification + editorial system is genuinely better, not just prettier.
- **Non-goal:** chasing OTA/commission revenue — it dissolves the moat (§6.4).

**Kill-signals to watch:** ChatGPT/Gemini shipping *artifact-quality, offline, private* trip documents (unlikely — misaligned with their engagement/commission incentives, but watch); an editorial brand (Wildsam, Monocle) partnering with an AI shop to do "guides for you."

## 9. One-line positioning candidates

- "A field guide, composed for you." (current spirit, keep)
- Against AI planners: *they answer questions; Excurse hands you a finished thing.*
- Against tools: *nothing to manage, nothing to sync, nothing to learn.*
- Against print: *it knows your name, your dates, and your kid's nap.*
- Against agencies: *the guide is the deliverable, and it's beautiful.*
